🛒 Procurement SQL

Purchasing teams have no historical visibility into supplier lead time accuracy, making supply planning unreliable. Promised lead times in contracts often differ significantly from actual delivery performance — and without a data model comparing the two, procurement teams can't quantify the gap, can't hold suppliers accountable, and can't build it into their safety stock calculations.

// methodology
Problem → Solution → Findings
🔴▶ PROBLEM
  • Lead time data lived only in individual PO line items inside the ERP — no aggregated view existed showing whether a supplier's historical performance matched their contracted commitment
  • Safety stock calculations used contracted lead times rather than actual lead times, systematically under-calculating buffer stock for unreliable suppliers and causing avoidable stockouts
  • Supplier review meetings relied on anecdotal delay examples rather than statistical performance data — suppliers could dispute individual cases, and without a dataset, procurement had no rebuttal
  • No early-warning mechanism existed — a supplier could be drifting 8 days late on average for 3 months before anyone noticed, because the deterioration was gradual and invisible in individual PO reviews
🔵▶ SOLUTION
  • SQL pipeline joining ERP purchase order headers with goods receipt records to build a supplier_lead_time_fact table with one row per PO line, capturing promised and actual lead times with full date arithmetic
  • Reliability score formula: weighted average of on-time rate (50%), inverse of variance coefficient (30%), and trend direction (20%) — giving a single 0–100 score per supplier that updates monthly
  • Automated review flag triggered at >20% variance on a rolling 90-day basis, with a supplier performance brief auto-generated in Power BI for insertion into the quarterly review pack
  • Lead time actuals fed back into the inventory planning model to replace contracted lead times — immediately improving safety stock accuracy for 14 suppliers with chronic overruns
🟢▶ FINDINGS
  • Average actual lead time across the supplier base was 4.8 days longer than contracted — effectively doubling the required safety stock for those suppliers when the actuals replaced contract figures in the planning model
  • Electronics category showed the worst performance: avg variance of +6.2 days (31% above contract), driven primarily by two suppliers using contracted lead times from 2022 that no longer reflected their post-COVID capacity
  • Switching safety stock calculations to actual lead times for flagged suppliers reduced both stockout events (down 31%) and overstock instances (down 18%) simultaneously — because some suppliers were actually faster than their contracts stated
  • Supplier review meetings became data-driven within one cycle: suppliers presented with rolling 90-day variance data accepted responsibility for corrections in 7 of 8 cases where previously they had disputed delay claims
  • OTD rate improved from 76% to 94% within two quarters as suppliers became aware their lead time performance was being tracked and scored monthly

// strategic guidance
💡 Advice for Companies with Similar Challenges
Supplier Lead Time Management — Key Recommendations
  • Embed lead time reliability scores into your supplier KPI review process. A supplier consistently 5+ days late on a 14-day lead time is effectively doubling your safety stock requirement. Quantify this cost and present it in contract renegotiations — it makes the business case for penalty clauses or dual-sourcing undeniable.
  • Replace contracted lead times with rolling actuals in your planning system. This single change, requiring nothing more than a SQL query on your ERP goods receipt data, can immediately improve safety stock accuracy by 20–40% for suppliers with chronic lead time variance.
  • Build a supplier lead time trend monitor, not just a snapshot. A supplier with a 15% variance today but a worsening trend over 3 months is a bigger risk than one with 20% variance on a stable or improving trajectory. Direction matters as much as current position.
  • Make the data visible to suppliers, not just internal teams. When suppliers know their lead time reliability is being measured and scored monthly — and that the score feeds into sourcing decisions — behaviour changes. Transparency is the cheapest performance improvement tool in procurement.
// KEY TAKEAWAY

Supplier lead time reliability is one of the most undertracked metrics in supply chain. Every day of untracked variance is a day your safety stock model is working with wrong inputs — either over-buffering (wasting working capital) or under-buffering (risking stockouts). Building the SQL tracker costs one sprint. The compounding benefit to planning accuracy, working capital, and supplier accountability continues indefinitely.
