Context
Problem

Manual replenishment at scale is failing

Planners were manually reviewing 847 SKUs weekly, spending 35 hours per planner on routine order decisions. Stockouts ran at 79 per quarter and excess stock tied up €1.2M in working capital due to inconsistent safety-stock policies.

Solution

AI Co-Pilot with confidence-gated automation

Built a Python-based AI engine combining XGBoost demand forecasts, dynamic ROP calculation, and a confidence scoring model. Orders above 85% confidence auto-approve; below that threshold, planners receive ranked recommendations with AI reasoning to review in minutes.

Findings

80% automation with 98% service level achieved

The system auto-approves 680 of 847 SKU replenishments weekly, cutting planner review time by 62%. Stockouts fell 71%, working capital released €284K, and service level climbed from 94.1% to 98.2% — validating the confidence-gate threshold.

Company Advice
⚡ For Leaders & Analysts Deploying AI Replenishment
  • Start with the confidence gate at 85% — do not set it higher than 90% until you have 90+ days of approval history to validate model drift.
  • Always log override reasons. After 30 overrides, mine the pattern: if 70%+ share a root cause (e.g. "promotional uplift"), feed that signal back into the model as a feature.
  • AI replenishment works best when demand forecasting MAPE is below 20%. Deploy the Forecast Accuracy Scorecard (Project 18) as a prerequisite diagnostic.
  • Safety stock set by a static formula will underperform a dynamic ML safety stock model — especially for lumpy or seasonal SKUs. Quantify the gap before go-live.
  • Planner buy-in is the biggest failure mode. Present the system as a co-pilot, not a replacement. Share the "AI was right" vs "Human was right" scorecard monthly.
Key Takeaway: Automation rate is a vanity metric — service level is what matters. An 80% automation rate achieving 98.2% service level outperforms 95% automation at 96% service level every time. Tune for outcomes, not throughput.
